# M1 $0 diagnostic — does retrieving the complete bridge set predict the answer?

Orchestrator-run, $0 (re-ran retrieval over the 100 pilot-answered questions; joined per-question
all-bridges@10 with the stored per-question answer-F1). Resolves the "dense retrieves better but answers
worse" puzzle before locking the comparator.

## Result 1 — complete-bridge retrieval is the dominant driver of answers
- all bridges in top-10  → mean answer-F1 = **0.510** (n=186 q-arm pairs)
- any bridge missing     → mean answer-F1 = **0.068** (n=114)
- **lift = +0.442** (7×). Retrieval predicts answering decisively.

## Result 2 — conditional on having all bridges, fused-RRF answers BEST; dense WORST
| arm | gets-all-bridges freq | F1 when all bridges present |
|---|---|---|
| bm25 | 0.51 | 0.498 |
| passage_dense | **0.68 (most)** | **0.464 (worst)** |
| fused-RRF | 0.65 | **0.552 (best)** |
| fused_rerank | 0.64 | 0.526 |

## Interpretation
Dense retrieves the bridges most often but buries them among semantically-similar **distractors**, so
the reader answers worst even with all bridges present. Fused-RRF's BM25∪dense mix yields the **cleanest
top-10 composition** → best answers. The CE rerank slightly hurts composition (0.526 < 0.552), demoting
bridges. So fused-RRF's marginal answer-F1 edge is a **real composition advantage, not noise** — it is the
legitimately strongest baseline on the registered endpoint (answer accuracy).

## Decision impact
Overturns the earlier lean toward dense-as-comparator. **Comparator = fused-RRF** (data-backed). Dense's
retrieval dominance + the composition effect are reported findings. Confirm MATERIAL_F1_LIFT = 0.04.
